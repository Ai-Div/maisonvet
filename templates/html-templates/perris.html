Water Rights

Municipal, not adjudicated

This property is served by Eastern Municipal Water District (EMWD), Riverside County's largest water, wastewater, and recycled water provider. Water is delivered via a municipal connection — meaning no private well rights and no senior groundwater position.

The underlying groundwater basin — the West San Jacinto Groundwater Basin — is not adjudicated. It is managed by EMWD as Groundwater Sustainability Agency (GSA) under California's Sustainable Groundwater Management Act (SGMA), with a Groundwater Sustainability Plan approved in 2023 and required to reach sustainability by 2042. Pumping is permitted within SGMA limits, but there are no quantified senior rights as there would be in a fully adjudicated basin.

What This Means Practically

  • —No drought-proof senior rights — SGMA can restrict basin pumping if overdraft is detected
  • —Municipal EMWD connection is reliable for residential use; rates are subject to change
  • —A private well can be installed, but output is governed by SGMA extraction limits
  • —This limits agricultural-scale irrigation — not a suitable base for a water-intensive crop farm
  • —Horse boarding uses modest water; adequate under EMWD service

How it works

Perris receives 5.5–6.3 kWh/m²/day of solar irradiance — among the highest in the US. The property has roughly 3.5–4 usable acres after the house footprint. Two paths exist:

Path 1 — Land lease to a developer

A solar developer installs and operates a ground-mount array on the available acreage. The landowner receives an annual lease payment with no upfront cost. California solar land lease rates range from $450–$2,500/acre/year depending on proximity to three-phase power lines and substation capacity. For ~3.5 acres in Riverside County: estimate $1,200–$2,300/acre/year = $4,400–$8,000/year passive income on a 25-year lease with 2% annual escalation. Emily and her mother can stay. The challenge: most utility-scale developers want 10+ acres. Smaller community solar and distributed generation developers work at the 3–5 acre scale, but require a nearby interconnection point (three-phase power line must be within ~500 feet).

Path 2 — Owner-installed system

Install a 75–150kW ground-mount array and use it to offset electricity bills for an on-site business. Paired with horse boarding or Airbnb operations, solar eliminates the electricity cost almost entirely. Under California's NEM 3.0 (April 2023), export credits dropped ~75% vs. NEM 2.0 — solar-only systems without battery storage now have a ~9-year payback. The better play is sizing the system to match consumption rather than to maximize grid exports.

Risk Factors — Know These Before Deciding

Fire Risk — Extreme

First Street Foundation rates this property at a 53% chance of being affected by wildfire in the next 30 years. This is not a minor consideration. California's homeowners insurance market is deteriorating rapidly — State Farm, Allstate, and Farmers have all restricted or exited new policy issuance in high-fire-risk areas. The FAIR Plan (California's insurer of last resort) is expensive and limited. A property with "Extreme" fire risk will face increasing insurance costs and eventual difficulty securing coverage entirely. This suppresses long-term value and poses real risk to the asset. Selling in the current hot market, before insurance availability further constrains buyer pools, is a legitimate strategy.

Capital Gains Tax

The property is assessed at $203,698 under Prop 13 — meaning it has likely been held for decades with very low tax. If the original purchase price was substantially below $600K, a sale will trigger capital gains tax on the difference. If Emily or the listed owner has lived in the property as a primary residence for 2 of the last 5 years, they may qualify for the Section 121 exclusion ($250K single / $500K married). A CPA must be consulted before listing. The specific cost basis and ownership structure determine the actual tax liability.

Short-Term Rental Compliance

Unincorporated Riverside County has an STR ordinance (Ordinance No. 927 as amended) requiring a Short-Term Rental Operating License and Transient Occupancy Tax registration. Operating without a permit can result in fines and forced closure. If Option D is pursued, obtain the permit before listing on any platform. Herbert St in Meadowbrook appears to be in unincorporated Riverside County, not within the City of Perris — confirm jurisdiction before applying.

Water Rights — No Senior Position

This property has no adjudicated water rights. It is on municipal EMWD service within the unadjudicated West San Jacinto Groundwater Basin, now governed by SGMA. EMWD's Groundwater Sustainability Plan was approved in 2023 with a 2042 sustainability deadline. Any private well installed on the property would be subject to SGMA extraction limits. This matters for agricultural use: water-intensive crops are not viable here without significant EMWD supply costs. Horse boarding is water-modest and remains viable. There is no risk to basic residential use.

Solar — Interconnection Is the Gate

The solar resource is exceptional, but the economics of a developer land lease hinge entirely on whether a three-phase SCE power line is within ~500 feet of the usable acreage. If the nearest interconnection point is further than that, the cost of line extension will eliminate developer interest. Before pursuing Option F, request a solar site feasibility assessment — US Light Energy, Nexamp, and Cypress Creek Renewables all operate in Riverside County at sub-10-acre scales. The assessment is typically free.
